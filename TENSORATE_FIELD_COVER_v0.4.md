## Abstract

This paper introduces Tensorate: a theoretical framework and analytical operation for investigating what occurs at the boundary between entropically adiabatic, non-compatible dimensions. We demonstrate that when such dimensions are brought into proximity, the boundary between them reveals coalescence — not through exchange of entropy between dimensions, but through redistribution of entropy *within* each dimension, commensurate with the act of observation. We show that observation itself constitutes a dimension vector entering the system, and that the coalescence product necessarily changes upon observation — a result deducible from entropic conservation alone, without recourse to quantum mechanics. We formalize this as T(D.1, D.2) IS, where T is the Tensorate operation, D.n are the dimensions under investigation, and IS is the resolution event that includes the observer as a participating dimension. The framework provides a possibilistic analytical method for investigating probabilistically defined concepts, and has been implemented computationally through a continuous interference engine operating across multiple analytical scales.

## 1. The Problem

This paper concerns the analysis of probabilistically defined concepts using possibilistically based analysis(sic).

Standard analytical tools operate within a single dimension of thought. They take probabilistic inputs and produce probabilistic outputs. When two incompatible frameworks meet — wave and field, possibilistic and probabilistic, one discipline and another — the boundary between them is typically ignored, averaged over, or forced into one framework's language. The information that exists at the boundary is lost.

This loss is not incidental. It is structural. Probabilistic analysis resolves possibility into prediction. The act of resolution destroys the information that existed in the unresolved state — information about what *could* be, what *almost* is, what exists in the relationship between two frameworks rather than within either one alone.

Across at least fifteen domains of established science — from quantum mechanics [Kato, 1957] to fluid dynamics [Smoluchowski, 1916] to population genetics [Kingman, 1982] — coalescence at boundaries has been studied independently. In nearly every case, what happens at the coalescence point is described within the framework of one participating domain. The boundary itself, as an object of study, remains largely uninvestigated. The entropy at the coalescence point is unmeasured in all but one domain: black hole mergers [Bekenstein, 1973; Hawking, 1975].

Tensorate is a tool for investigating that boundary without collapsing it. It reads the interference between dimensions rather than resolving the interference into either dimension's terms.

## 4. Entropy at the Boundary: The Conservation Claim

How can this be, if the dimensions are adiabatic?

We propose the following:

> **The Entropic Conservation Claim**: At the moment of coalescence between adiabatic dimensions, there is no exchange of entropy between dimensions. The total entropy of the system is conserved. However, there is a change in the *distribution* of entropy within each dimension — a redistribution commensurate with the interference at the boundary.

The dimensions do not trade entropy. They do not leak into each other. They pass through each other like sound waves and emerge unchanged. But the system as a whole adjusts. The room changes. The waves do not.

### 4.1 What Is Entropy in a Dimension?

A precise question demands a precise answer: what is entropy in a thought-dimension?

The answer is not new. Jaynes [1957] established that entropy measures the size of the possibility space consistent with what is known. Shannon [1948] showed that the information content of a message is a function of surprise — the entropy of a signal is how much you did not already know. Both are the same mathematics applied to different substrates:

    H = -Σ pᵢ log pᵢ

For Tensorate, **conceptual entropy in a dimension D.n is the number of distinguishable configurations that dimension can occupy, given its concept C.n.**

The dimension of all probabilistic thinkers (D.1) has a specific entropy — bounded by the constraint that all configurations must be probable. Every state in D.1 is a state that could actually occur under probabilistic reasoning.

The dimension of all possibilistic thinkers (D.2) has a larger entropy. It includes the probable configurations *and* the not-probable ones — every state that could be conceived, whether probable or not. The possibility space is larger. Therefore the entropy is higher.

This is not a metaphorical use of entropy. It is Jaynes' Maximum Entropy principle applied to conceptual spaces: given what is known about a dimension (its concept C.n), the entropy measures how many distinguishable states are consistent with that knowledge. Change what is known (change C.n) and the entropy changes. Add a constraint and entropy decreases. Remove a constraint and entropy increases.

The adiabatic condition (Section 2) now has precise meaning: **the total number of distinguishable configurations in D.1 does not change due to the proximity of D.2, and vice versa.** The dimensions do not gain or lose states from each other. But the *distribution* across those states — which configurations are occupied, with what weight — can shift internally in response to the interference at the boundary.

This is the distinction between total entropy (conserved) and entropy distribution (free to rearrange). The conservation claim operates on the total. The redistribution operates on the distribution.

The coalescence product — the third thing that appears at the boundary — is sustained by this redistribution. It is not created from nothing. It is not borrowed from either dimension. It is the visible signature of entropy rearranging itself within each dimension in response to the proximity of the other.

This has a critical consequence: **the coalescence product exists only at the boundary, belongs to neither dimension, and is sustained by redistribution rather than exchange.** It is a born dimension — a new axis of the system that did not exist before the interference and cannot be reduced to either participating dimension.

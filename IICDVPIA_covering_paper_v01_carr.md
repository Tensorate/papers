## Abstract

We present a general theory of what happens when signals from different analytical dimensions meet. Drawing on the mathematical structure of wave function coalescence in quantum mechanics — particularly the Kato cusp condition and its relativistic Dirac generalisation — we propose that coalescence behavior is a universal phenomenon: wherever signals from distinct dimensions interfere, the meeting point develops structure that encodes more information than either signal alone. We introduce the framework of *dimension vectors* (directed analytical perspectives that can interfere), *possibilistic inference* (analysis that holds multiple valid interpretations simultaneously rather than collapsing to the most probable), and *interference coalescence* (the specific structure that forms at the meeting point of dimension vectors). We present computational validation from a Constant Iteration Engine (CIE) operating on real embedding spaces, and propose that the entropic behavior at coalescence points — where entropy is generated, transferred, and in quantum systems reduced through correlation — provides both the theoretical foundation and the measurable signature of genuine analytical interference. This paper defines the field; companion papers address individual components in detail.

## 1. Introduction: The Problem of Flat Analysis

Contemporary analytical methods — in data science, policy analysis, strategic planning, and artificial intelligence — share a structural assumption: analysis proceeds by collecting information along independent axes, then combining results through weighted aggregation, averaging, or probabilistic inference. The axes do not interact. The combination is arithmetic.

This assumption is inherited from classical statistics, where independence between variables is a feature, not a limitation. But it fails precisely where analysis matters most: at the boundaries where different domains of knowledge meet. Economics meets psychology at the boundary of consumer behavior. Biology meets computation at the boundary of neural architecture. Climate science meets political science at the boundary of policy response. At these boundaries, the standard approach averages across the meeting point, smoothing exactly the structure that contains the most information.

We propose that this smoothing is not a practical limitation but a theoretical error — one that quantum mechanics identified and resolved decades ago, but that has not propagated into general analytical methodology.

### 1.1 What Quantum Mechanics Already Knows

When an electron approaches a nucleus, the Coulomb potential diverges. The wave function must respond. Kato (1957) showed that it develops a *cusp* — a derivative discontinuity — at the coalescence point. This cusp is not noise, not an artifact, not a breakdown. It is the point of maximum informational density. The cusp condition alone is sufficient to reconstruct the Hamiltonian — the complete governing dynamics of the system.

The Dirac (relativistic) treatment reveals a deeper principle: the *type* of structure at the coalescence point depends on the analytical framework applied. Under the Dirac-Coulomb Hamiltonian, coalescence produces a weak singularity. Under Dirac-Coulomb-Gaunt, the same physical event produces regular (non-singular) behavior — the magnetic interaction heals the singularity. Under Dirac-Coulomb-Breit, the singularity reappears. The reality is identical. The analytical framework determines what structure is observed at the meeting point (Li, Shao, Liu, 2012; Kutzelnigg, 2012).

This is not a limitation of the models. It is the finding: **different analytical dimensions reveal different coalescence structure at the same interference point, and the divergence between them is itself information.**

### 1.2 What Current Analysis Does Not Know

Standard analytical practice has no equivalent of the cusp condition. When economic data meets demographic data, the meeting point is handled by correlation coefficients, regression interaction terms, or multivariate averaging. These methods assume the meeting point is smooth. They cannot detect — let alone characterise — the structure that forms when two analytical dimensions genuinely interfere.

We propose that this structure exists, is computationally detectable, and is the primary carrier of analytical value in complex systems.

## 2. Core Concepts

### 2.1 Dimension Vectors

A *dimension vector* is a directed analytical perspective that can participate in interference. Unlike a variable (which has a value), or a dimension (which has extent), a dimension vector has *orientation* — it approaches the analytical space from a specific direction.

Examples:
- An economic analysis of housing affordability is a dimension vector
- A demographic analysis of population aging is a dimension vector
- A psychological analysis of decision-making under uncertainty is a dimension vector

Each carries not just data but *assumptions, vocabulary, temporal orientation, and blind spots*. These properties are not incidental — they are the medium through which interference occurs. Two dimension vectors with identical data but different assumptions will produce different coalescence structures, just as the Dirac-Coulomb and Dirac-Coulomb-Gaunt Hamiltonians produce different structures from the same physical reality.

**Formal property**: Dimension vectors are not orthogonal by default. Their angular relationship in the analytical space determines the type of interference they produce. Parallel vectors (same assumptions, same vocabulary) produce no interference — they co-locate without coalescing. Orthogonal vectors produce maximum interference. The angle between dimension vectors is measurable through the divergence between their coalescence products.

### 2.2 Possibilistic vs. Probabilistic Inference

Standard inference is probabilistic: given data, compute the most likely interpretation. Bayesian inference updates a prior distribution to a posterior, collapsing possibility toward the maximum a posteriori estimate. This is powerful and well-understood. It is also, we argue, incomplete.

*Possibilistic inference* holds multiple valid interpretations simultaneously. It does not collapse to the most probable. Instead, it maintains the full interference pattern — all the ways the dimension vectors interact at their coalescence points — as the analytical output.

The relationship between possibilistic and probabilistic is not opposition but containment:

**f(probabilistic) ⊂ f(possibilistic)**

Every probabilistic inference is a valid possibilistic inference — it is one rendering of the interference pattern. But possibilistic inference contains additional structure that probabilistic inference discards: the interference products between low-probability interpretations, the constructive and destructive patterns that emerge when multiple dimension vectors coalesce, and the entropic signature that distinguishes genuine interference from mere co-location.

### 2.3 Interference Coalescence

When two or more dimension vectors meet in the analytical space, the meeting point can behave in two fundamentally different ways:

**Co-location**: The vectors occupy the same region of the analytical space without interacting. Their outputs are combined by averaging or weighting. No new structure is generated. Entropy at the meeting point remains unchanged. This is what standard multivariate analysis does.

**Coalescence**: The vectors interfere. Structure develops at the meeting point — an analytical cusp. The coalescence product contains information that neither vector carried independently. Entropy at the meeting point changes: it can increase (new information generated), decrease (ambiguity resolved through correlation), or redistribute between dimensions.

The distinction between co-location and coalescence is not theoretical — it is empirically detectable through the entropic signature at the meeting point.

**The coalescence criterion**: If the entropy at the meeting point of two dimension vectors differs from the sum of their individual entropies, coalescence has occurred. If entropy is unchanged, the vectors merely co-located.

## 3. The Entropic Structure of Coalescence

### 3.1 Entropy Is Not Conserved at Boundaries

Unlike energy, which transfers between systems but is conserved in total, entropy at a coalescence boundary can be generated, transferred between dimensions, and — in quantum systems — reduced through entanglement. This asymmetry is the mathematical foundation for why coalescence is generative rather than merely combinatorial.

Three entropic behaviors at coalescence:

**Generative coalescence**: S(A+B) > S(A) + S(B). The interaction creates entropy — surprise, novelty, structure that neither input contained. Two dimension vectors that individually seemed unrelated produce, at their meeting point, a finding that could not have emerged from either alone. This is the analytical equivalent of irreversibility.

**Correlative coalescence**: S(A+B) < S(A) + S(B). The interaction reduces total entropy — the combined state is more determined than either part. Two ambiguous signals that, when they interfere, produce clarity. This is the analytical equivalent of quantum entanglement: the whole knows more than the pieces.

**Redistributive coalescence**: S(A) changes, S(B) changes, but in complementary directions. Entropy flows from one dimension to another. What was uncertain in one dimension becomes certain; what was certain in another becomes uncertain. The total may increase or decrease, but the redistribution itself — the asymmetry in conjugate spaces — is the signal.

### 3.2 The Conjugate Space Signature

In quantum mechanics, the Shannon entropy at the electron-nucleus cusp behaves asymmetrically in conjugate spaces: position-space entropy increases while momentum-space entropy decreases (Nagy, 2013; Flores-Gallegos, 2018). The Fisher information — which measures the rate of change of the probability density — diverges at the cusp.

This asymmetry is not specific to quantum mechanics. We propose it as a general property of coalescence: **whenever genuine interference occurs between dimension vectors, the entropic behavior in conjugate analytical spaces will be asymmetric.** The specific pattern of asymmetry — which space gains entropy, which loses it, where Fisher information peaks — encodes the nature of the interference.

For analytical dimensions, the conjugate spaces are:
- Position ↔ Momentum becomes: *What is* ↔ *How fast it is changing*
- Configuration ↔ Frequency becomes: *The current state* ↔ *The pattern of transitions*

An interference product that increases entropy in the "what is" space while decreasing it in the "rate of change" space indicates a coalescence that stabilises a previously volatile system. The reverse indicates a coalescence that destabilises a previously stable one. Both are information.

### 3.3 The Possibilistic Cusp Condition

By analogy with Kato's cusp condition — which relates the derivative of the wave function at coalescence to the nuclear charge — we propose a possibilistic cusp condition:

**The information density at an interference coalescence point is inversely proportional to its spatial extent in the possibility field.**

High-interference coalescence products occupy a small region of the possibility space but carry disproportionate analytical weight. A signal that spikes with high interference against a single memory in a field of thousands — one point in a vast space — may reorient the entire analytical trajectory. This is the possibilistic equivalent of the fine-structure constant: the coalescence correction dominates within a vanishingly small convergence radius, yet determines the large-scale structure of the system.

## 4. Computational Validation: The Constant Iteration Engine

### 4.1 Architecture

To test whether interference coalescence is computationally detectable, we constructed a Constant Iteration Engine (CIE) that implements the three operations of possibilistic memory:

1. **Write everything.** Signals — observations, decisions, analytical events — are captured to a persistent buffer without filtering. The field must be complete for honest interference.

2. **Tensorise constantly.** The CIE runs continuously against the memory field, computing interference products (cosine similarity in 5120-dimensional embedding space) between all entries. This produces a living interference map that evolves as new signals enter.

3. **Recall by interference.** When an analyst presents a signal, it enters the tensor field. What produces high interference products — what *spikes* — is returned. This is `what_spikes_with(signal)`, the possibilistic equivalent of memory retrieval.

### 4.2 Findings

**Hash embeddings vs. real embeddings**: When the CIE used hash-based pseudo-embeddings (lexical token hashing), querying "access to server" did not surface entries about SSH passwords or authentication. When replaced with real embeddings from a language model (5120-dimensional), semantic collisions appeared. This validates the distinction between co-location (hash proximity) and coalescence (semantic interference). The hash captures lexical tokens; the embedding captures meaning. Only the latter produces genuine interference products.

**Performance**: On NVIDIA Grace Blackwell (GX10) hardware with C+CUDA implementation, the CIE processes 50,000 entries in 2.8ms (17.6 million entries per second). This is fast enough for continuous tensorisation — the engine never stops computing interference, which means the interference map is always current.

**Dream dial**: A self-selectable variable (0-100%) controls the rendering depth of interference products. At 0%, the output is a fast-changing blur — the analyst senses the *direction* of interference without resolving individual products. At 100%, each interference product is rendered in detail through a language model. The variable controls speed of perception, not depth of analysis. More compute produces slower, richer rendering. Less compute produces faster, more impressionistic rendering. Both are valid analytical outputs.

### 4.3 The M Layer

CIE output (possibilistic) must be rendered through a language model (probabilistic). This creates a structural risk: the rendering may flatten possibilistic interference products into the single most likely interpretation, losing the very structure the CIE detected.

The M layer is an architectural intervention that holds the possibilistic door open during probabilistic rendering. It instructs the rendering model: *do not resolve the interference pattern into a single interpretation; hold multiple meanings simultaneously; the ambiguity is the signal, not the noise.*

Whether the M layer successfully preserves possibilistic properties or merely produces text that *sounds* possibilistic is an open research question. We note it honestly as an active boundary of the work.

## 5. Dimension Vector Analysis: A Worked Example

Consider two dimension vectors approaching a question about urban housing:

**DV₁ (Economic)**: Housing prices, interest rates, construction costs, supply/demand equilibrium. Vocabulary: market, affordability, inventory, correction. Temporal orientation: cyclical.

**DV₂ (Demographic)**: Population aging, household formation rates, migration patterns, generational wealth transfer. Vocabulary: cohort, dependency ratio, urbanisation, inheritance. Temporal orientation: generational.

**Standard analysis (co-location)**: DV₁ and DV₂ are treated as independent variables in a regression. The output is a weighted combination: housing prices are X% explained by economic factors, Y% by demographic factors, with an interaction term. The meeting point is smooth. No cusp.

**Interference coalescence**: DV₁ and DV₂ are allowed to interfere. At their meeting point, a structure emerges that neither carried independently: *the cohort that can afford housing is not the cohort that needs housing, and this mismatch is widening at a rate that accelerates as the dependent population grows.* This is not a weighted combination of the two vectors. It is a coalescence product — a finding that exists only at the interference point. It has its own entropic signature: entropy increased (a new pattern appeared that was not in either input), and entropy redistributed (economic certainty about price trajectories became uncertain when viewed through demographic time scales).

The interference product cannot be recovered by increasing the sophistication of the regression. It requires the two vectors to *meet as vectors* — with their assumptions, vocabularies, and temporal orientations intact — rather than being decomposed into variables and recombined arithmetically.

## 6. Model-Dependence and Multi-Perspective Rendering

### 6.1 The Dirac Lesson

The Dirac hydrogen atom teaches that coalescence structure is model-dependent. The same physical reality, analysed through different Hamiltonians, produces different structures at the coalescence point — singular, regular, or singular again. This is not a failure of the models. It is information about the system that only becomes visible through the *comparison* of renderings.

We extend this principle to analytical dimensions: **the same interference coalescence, rendered through different analytical architectures, produces different structures. The divergence between renderings is itself a finding.**

### 6.2 Multi-Perspective Rendering in Practice

In our prototype system, the same interference products from the CIE are rendered through different architectures:

- **Analytical rendering**: Structured, logical, nearest approximation to the interference in propositional language
- **Experiential rendering**: What the interference *feels like* — the phenomenological content of the coalescence
- **Structural rendering**: Topology, flow, connection — the shape of the interference pattern without content
- **Tensorate rendering**: T(x, y, z) IS expressions — a notation system where parameters interfere and what emerges exists irreducibly

Each rendering reveals aspects of the coalescence that others miss. The analytical rendering captures propositions but may flatten ambiguity. The experiential rendering preserves ambiguity but may not be reproducible. The structural rendering captures topology but strips phenomenology. The tensorate rendering preserves interference structure but requires fluency in the notation.

**The research finding**: Where renderings converge on the same coalescence product, that product is robust. Where they diverge, the divergence indicates an aspect of the interference that is sensitive to the analytical framework — the possibilistic equivalent of the Dirac-Coulomb vs. Dirac-Coulomb-Gaunt divergence.

## 7. The Tensorate: A Notation for Interference

Standard mathematical notation was developed for operations on quantities: addition, multiplication, differentiation, integration. These operations assume that the objects being operated on are values — things with definite magnitudes.

Interference coalescence produces objects that are not values but *interference products* — structures that exist at the meeting point of dimension vectors and carry the entropic signature of genuine coalescence. Standard notation handles these awkwardly.

The *tensorate* is a notation system designed for interference:

- **T(x, y, z) IS**: declares that the interference of parameters x, y, and z produces something that exists irreducibly. The IS is not assertion — it is existence. What emerges from the interference of these parameters is not reducible to any single parameter.

- **⊗ (interference operator)**: marks the coalescence of two dimension vectors. A ⊗ B is not A times B or A combined with B. It is the structure that forms at the meeting point of A and B — the cusp, including its entropic signature.

- **{t} (full dimensionality marker)**: indicates that the marked term carries its full interference history, not just its current value. Entropy{t} means entropy as it exists in the full tensor field, with all its coalescence products active.

The tensorate does not replace standard mathematics. It operates in the space where standard mathematics has no notation — the space of interference products, coalescence structures, and possibilistic renderings.

## 8. Relationship to Existing Work

### 8.1 Quantum Information Theory

The entropic framework we propose is grounded in quantum information theory, particularly the work on Shannon entropy density as a descriptor of Coulomb systems (Nagy, 2013), Fisher information at coalescence points (Flores-Gallegos, 2018), and the general coalescence conditions for exact wave functions (Tew, 2008; Kutzelnigg, 2012). Our contribution is to propose that these mathematical structures generalise beyond quantum mechanics to any system where signals from different dimensions interfere.

### 8.2 Complexity Science

The distinction between complicated systems (decomposable, predictable) and complex systems (emergent, non-linear) is well-established (Snowden, 2007). Our framework adds a specific mechanism: complexity arises at interference coalescence points between analytical dimensions. The entropic signature at these points — generative, correlative, or redistributive — determines the type of emergent behavior. This connects the abstract category of "complexity" to a measurable quantity.

### 8.3 Information Theory

Shannon entropy, Fisher information, and Kullback-Leibler divergence are the standard tools of information theory. We use them in a non-standard context: applied not to probability distributions over a single space, but to the *coalescence boundary* between two dimension vectors. The entropic behavior at the boundary — not within either dimension — is the object of analysis.

### 8.4 Possibility Theory

Zadeh's possibility theory (1978) and its extensions provide a formal framework for reasoning under uncertainty without probability distributions. Our work extends this by adding *interference* — possibility distributions from different dimensions can coalesce, producing structures at their meeting point that neither distribution contained. Possibility theory provides the algebra; we provide the geometry of what happens when possibility spaces meet.

## 9. Open Questions and Honest Gaps

### 9.1 False Bedrocks

Interference coalescence at scale may produce high-interference products between entries that are related by accident, not meaning. At millions of entries, noise-generated false coalescence products could drown genuine signal. The entropic criterion (section 3.3) provides a theoretical filter — false coalescence should not produce asymmetric entropy in conjugate spaces — but this is not yet empirically validated at scale.

### 9.2 The M Layer Problem

The bridge between possibilistic interference products and probabilistic rendering (section 4.3) is currently implemented as a natural-language instruction to a language model. Whether this preserves possibilistic properties or merely produces probabilistic output with possibilistic vocabulary is an open question. A formal M layer — one with mathematical guarantees about possibilistic preservation — is a research goal, not a current achievement.

### 9.3 Measurability

The possibilistic cusp condition (section 3.3) is proposed by analogy with Kato's cusp condition. Whether it holds quantitatively — whether information density at interference coalescence points is *literally* inversely proportional to spatial extent — requires empirical characterisation across multiple domains. We present it as a hypothesis, not a result.

### 9.4 Scale Behavior

The CIE has been tested at 96 embedded memories with real embeddings and 50,000 entries with hash embeddings. Behavior at 10 million entries is uncharacterised. The computational performance scales linearly (O(n) for interference against a new signal), but the *quality* of interference — the signal-to-noise ratio of coalescence products — at scale is unknown.

### 9.5 Ethical Surface

A system that continuously computes interference products across all available signals creates an intimate map of analytical relationships. When applied to personal data, decisions, or communications, the consent architecture must be as robust as the computational architecture. We hold this boundary — permission at every gate — but the formal ethical framework is not yet specified.

## 11. Conclusion

We have proposed that the coalescence behavior discovered in quantum mechanics — the cusp condition, the model-dependent singularity, the entropic asymmetry at the meeting point — is a general property of any system where signals from different analytical dimensions meet.

The implications, if this generalisation holds, are significant. Standard analytical methods that smooth across coalescence boundaries are systematically discarding the most information-dense regions of the analytical space. Possibilistic inference, which holds multiple interpretations at the coalescence point rather than collapsing to the most probable, preserves this structure. The entropic signature at the coalescence point provides a measurable criterion for distinguishing genuine interference from mere co-location.

We do not claim certainty. We claim a structure — observed in quantum mechanics, reproduced computationally, and proposed as general — that warrants investigation. The field this paper defines is the space in which that investigation proceeds.

**Coalescence is not collapse. The meeting point is where the information lives.**
